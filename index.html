Why Does Phase Matter?

Every digital filter has two faces: its magnitude response (how much it boosts or attenuates each frequency) and its phase response (how much it delays each frequency). Two filters can share the exact same magnitude response yet sound and behave completely differently, because their phase responses differ.

The key insight is that for any given magnitude response, there are many filters that can produce it. Among all of them, the minimum-phase filter is special: it concentrates energy earliest in time and has the smallest total phase shift. The maximum-phase filter is its mirror — it pushes energy as late as possible.

Key Concept

A causal, stable filter's phase behaviour is entirely determined by where its zeros sit relative to the unit circle in the z-plane. Zeros inside → minimum phase. Zeros outside → maximum phase.

The Z-Plane: Poles, Zeros & the Unit Circle

A discrete-time transfer function H(z) is a ratio of polynomials in z. The roots of the numerator are zeros and the roots of the denominator are poles. The unit circle |z| = 1 is where we evaluate the frequency response.

H(z) = b₀ · ∏(z − zₖ) / ∏(z − pₖ)

Minimum-Phase vs Maximum-Phase

Given a zero at position z₀, we can reflect it to 1/z₀* (its conjugate-reciprocal) without changing the magnitude response. But the phase and impulse response change dramatically.

Allpass Decomposition

Any causal stable filter can be decomposed into a minimum-phase component cascaded with an allpass filter:

H(z) = H_min(z) · H_ap(z)

The allpass filter has magnitude 1 everywhere — it only adds phase. This means the minimum-phase version has exactly the same magnitude response as the original, but with the least possible phase lag.

Practical Significance

Minimum-phase filters are prized in audio, control systems, and geophysics because they are invertible. If a channel or system is minimum-phase, you can perfectly undo its effect with a causal, stable inverse filter. Maximum-phase components require non-causal processing to invert.

Group Delay

Group delay is the negative derivative of phase with respect to frequency: τ(ω) = −dφ/dω. It tells you how much each frequency component is delayed by the filter. Minimum-phase filters have the smallest total group delay for a given magnitude response.

Mixed-Phase & Real-World Filters

Most real-world systems are mixed-phase: they contain some zeros inside and some outside the unit circle. The allpass decomposition still works — the minimum-phase factor gathers all zeros inside, and the allpass factor accounts for the rest.

Where This Matters

Audio & Acoustics: Room impulse responses are typically mixed-phase. Minimum-phase equalisation is used to correct magnitude without adding latency.

Seismology: The earth's impulse response is assumed minimum-phase in deconvolution algorithms used to locate oil and gas reserves.

Control Systems: Non-minimum-phase plants (with right-half-plane zeros in continuous time) impose fundamental limits on achievable bandwidth.

Communications: Channel equalisation exploits minimum-phase structure for efficient, causal inverse filtering.

Summary

Minimum-phase means all zeros inside the unit circle — giving minimum phase lag, minimum group delay, front-loaded energy, and a stable causal inverse. Maximum-phase is the opposite extreme. Understanding where zeros sit is the key to understanding how a filter shapes signals in time.
